Stream Vision II Overview

Stream Vision II is a revolutionary application built for hunters, nature observers, and security professionals who rely on the latest Yukon Advanced Optics devices. The product delivers a single, calm user experience across iOS, Android, and the web, giving 90,000 daily users remote control over scopes, cameras, and video streams.

The platform blends Lean UX and Agile UX practices: continuous research, iterative prototyping, and post-release analysis keep the interface responsive to real field conditions.

Every release cycle focused on sharpening the live viewfinder, media workflows, and remote commands—areas that most directly affect users in the field.

Goals

The primary objective was to deliver a unified design that scales across platforms and device sizes while honoring the needs of hunters, naturalists, and security teams.

To accomplish this, we delivered:

  • Intuitive viewfinder interfaces for real-time observation.
  • Controls for recording, streaming, and scope management.
  • Lightweight video editing tools for quick stories.
  • Robust content organization and search.
  • And a continuous roadmap for new optical devices.

Process

We combined Lean UX with Agile UX to keep delivery fast without losing depth. Research visits to factories and shooting ranges helped us understand how people actually use the hardware, while competitor reviews and CJMs guided the backlog.

The development process had two phases:

  • Pre-release prototyping and testing: iterative design, usability studies, and QA to stabilize the core flows.
  • Post-release improvements: adding features, refining automation, and responding to analytics and feedback.

Strategy

At the strategy stage we aligned goals, requirements, and technical constraints. Workshops with stakeholders, factory walk-throughs, and scope demos surfaced critical use cases and inspired our earliest hypotheses.

We audited indirect competitors, mapped the journeys of four personas, and produced documentation that guided design and engineering as the interface matured.

Discovery

Close collaboration with Pulsar’s marketing team gave us direct access to thousands of users. We drafted surveys for 1,000 respondents, focusing on expectations for the new product and frustrations with the legacy app.

We reviewed App Store, Google Play, Twitter, and Reddit feedback to avoid repeating competitor mistakes.

Sketching

Key research findings were translated into native UI concepts for iOS and Android. We leveraged an internal framework to assemble trustworthy prototypes rapidly.

Five interactive Figma prototypes anchored stakeholder demos and captured feedback before we invested in polishing visuals.

Usability Testing

We ran remote sessions via Google Meet and in-person focus groups to validate critical flows: first-time device connection, content management, file transfers, cloud login, and scope control.

Test scenarios included:

  • Connecting a device for the first time.
  • Deleting, searching, filtering, and editing clips.
  • Transferring files between storage locations.
  • Logging into cloud storage.
  • Managing device modes, PiP, scopes, and firmware.

File transfer flows posed the biggest challenge, especially on Android. After 15 rounds of tests we simplified the storage model, reducing handoffs and improving task completion dramatically.

UI Design

A robust UI kit evolved into a full design system for both platforms. With marketing and graphic design partners, we ensured the look and feel aligned with Yukon’s brand book while honoring native patterns.

Native components were replicated to create platform-specific styles, covering colors, typography, and interaction patterns.

The Viewfinder section demanded special care—it needed to mirror the physical controls of scopes and binoculars. We A/B tested concepts, iterated with device designers, and reduced functionality to the most essential states to keep the experience calm.

Preparation for Development

With the design system and key flows solid, we partnered with the BA to document every interaction, component logic, and palette rule. Workshops with engineering teams resolved edge cases before handoff.

Post-Release Work

For two years post-launch we focused on new functionality, UX refinements, and instrumentation in Amplitude. We tracked retention, screen time, feature adoption, user paths, and task completion.

  • Brainstorming sessions and A/B tests to improve key metrics.
  • Review mining in app stores to surface expectations.

When users disliked the viewfinder icons, we redesigned them to mirror physical devices, boosting satisfaction across regions.

Project Development

We expanded the ecosystem to tablets, smartwatches, experimental AR glasses, and the Stream Vision Cloud web app, ensuring parity across contexts.

Another spin-off, Stream Vision Ballistics, helped hunters calculate trajectories; field visits and range tests ensured it matched on-the-ground workflows.

Results and Conclusions

Over three years, the team achieved an 88-point design satisfaction score and high app store ratings. The design system enables rapid UI hypotheses, reducing task completion times by ~35%.

Continuous testing let us fix usability issues before launch, and the system we built continues to guide new designers and developers.
